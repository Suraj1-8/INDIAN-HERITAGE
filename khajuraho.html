Khajuraho Temples

Overview

The Khajuraho Group of Monuments is a collection of Hindu and Jain temples in Chhatarpur district, Madhya Pradesh, India. These temples are famous for their intricate sculptures and architectural excellence. Built between 950 and 1050 CE by the Chandela dynasty, the temples are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and represent one of the finest examples of Indian architectural heritage.

Quick Facts

  • Built: 950-1050 CE
  • Dynasty: Chandela
  • Style: Nagara Style Architecture
  • Location: Chhatarpur, Madhya Pradesh

Visiting Hours

  • Open: All days
  • Timing: Sunrise to Sunset
  • Light & Sound Show: Evening (in English and Hindi)
  • Entry Fee: ₹40 (Indian), ₹600 (Foreign)

History

The Khajuraho temples were built during the reign of the Chandela dynasty, which ruled over central India from the 9th to the 13th centuries. Originally, there were 85 temples spread over an area of 20 square kilometers, but only 25 have survived to the present day. The temples were built in three phases, with the earliest temples dating back to 950 CE.

The temples were rediscovered by a British army engineer, T.S. Burt, in 1838. The name "Khajuraho" is derived from the Sanskrit word "Kharjuravahaka," which means "one who carries scorpions." The temples were built to showcase the power and wealth of the Chandela dynasty and to serve as places of worship and learning.

Architecture

The Khajuraho temples are built in the Nagara style of architecture, which is characterized by its curvilinear towers (shikharas) and intricate sculptures. The temples are divided into three groups: Western, Eastern, and Southern. The Western group, which includes the Kandariya Mahadeva Temple, is the most famous and well-preserved.

Key Features

  • Curvilinear Towers (Shikharas)
  • Intricate Sculptures
  • Mandapa (Pillared Hall)
  • Ardhamandapa (Entrance Porch)
  • Garbhagriha (Sanctum Sanctorum)
  • Jagati (Raised Platform)

Materials Used

  • Sandstone
  • Granite
  • Limestone
  • Marble (for some sculptures)

How to Reach

By Air

Khajuraho has its own airport with regular flights from Delhi, Varanasi, and other major cities. The airport is about 5 km from the temple complex.

By Train

The nearest railway station is Mahoba (about 75 km away). You can take a bus or taxi from there to Khajuraho. Alternatively, you can take a train to Jhansi (175 km away) and then travel by road.
